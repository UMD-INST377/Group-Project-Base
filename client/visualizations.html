Collisions by Type

This box chart shows what type of impact is recorded at the time of the accident. From the data we can see that a vast majority of the accidents are same direction rear-ends.

A report from the National Transportation Safety Board found that 87% of rear-end collisions happened because of a distracted driver.

Driver distraction is by far the leading cause of this type of crash, as a driver does not notice the vehicle in front of them in time to slow down or stop to avoid a collision.

The doughnut chart to the right shows driver culpability or responsibility for fault in an accident.

According to National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, for more than two decades, speeding has been involved in approximately one-third of all motor vehicle fatalities. In 2019, speeding was a contributing factor in 26% of all traffic fatalities.

Collisions by Age and Sex

The radar graph shows the number of collisions separated by both age and sex of the drivers.

While not representative on the graph, The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration reports that men cause an average of 6.1 million accidents per year in the U.S., and women cause 4.4 million accidents per year.

Drivers ages 16-17 continue to have the highest rates of crash involvement, injuries to themselves and others and deaths of others in crashes in which they are involved. Drivers age 80 and older have the highest rates of driver deaths.
